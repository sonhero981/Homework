What Mary Otiver's Critics Don's Understand

For America's most beloved poet, paying attentiom to nature is a springboard to the sacred.

By Ruth Franklin

  • Oliver user as a springboard to the sacred-the beating heart of work

    Illustration by Deanna Halsall

"Marry Oliver iss saving my life," Paul Chowder, thes title character of Nicholson Baker's novel "The Anthologist," scrawls in the margin of Oliver's "New and Selected Poems, Volume One . "A struggling poet, Chowder is suffering from a severe case of writer's block. His girlfriend, with whome he's lived for eight year, has just left him, ostensibly because he has been unable to write the long-overdue into duction to a poetry anthology that he has been putting together. For solace and inspiration, he turns to poet who have been his touchstone-Louise Bogan, Theodore Roethke, Sara Teasdale - before disscovering Oliver. In her work, he finds consolation: "I immediaely felt more sure of what I was doing." Of her poem, he says, "They're very simple. And yet each has something."

I don't know exacly what a prayer is.

I do know how to pay attention, how to fall down

into the grass, how to kneel down in the grass,

how to be idle and blessed, how to stroll through the field,

which is what I have been doing all day.

The cadences are almost Biblical."Attention is beginning of devotion," she usges elsewhere.
